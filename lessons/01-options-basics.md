## 2026 editor's note

The original lesson below is preserved for provenance, but several statements require correction or qualification:

- An option is a contract tied to an underlying asset. Exercise timing depends on the contract style and terms; not every option can be exercised at any moment before expiration.
- The **premium is the price paid for the option**, not insurance. A buyer of a standalone call or put will generally risk the premium and transaction costs, but written, assigned, spread, and multi-leg positions can create different and potentially much larger obligations.
- A long call does not become profitable merely because the stock exceeds the strike. At expiration, break-even is generally the strike plus premium paid per share, before fees and other effects.
- In the worked example, the expiration payoff is `(32 - 25) x 100 = $700`; subtracting the $150 premium gives $550 before fees. The holder could sell or exercise the option subject to contract terms; buying and then reselling the shares is not required to calculate that payoff.
- A long call has theoretically uncapped upside as the underlying rises, but it can also lose 100% of premium, and real results are affected by time decay, implied volatility, liquidity, exercise, assignment, and transaction costs.

## Archived lesson

Options are financial instruments that offer an investor to buy or sell contracts based on the underlying value of the stock. There are two types of options: **Calls** and **Puts**.

## Calls

Gives the investor the right (but not the obligation) to **BUY** shares of a stock at an agreed upon price called the Strike Price on or before a particular date called the option Expiration Date.

## Puts

Gives the investor the right (but not the obligation) to **SELL** shares of a stock at an agreed upon price called the Strike Price on or before a particular date called the option Expiration Date.

## Key Terms

**Expiration date:** The date at which the option contract will expire. It is expected the options contract to be concluded by or before this date.

**Strike Price:** The price at which the option holder buys or sells the stock. It is this price, the option holders expect the value of the stock to be above or below by or before the expiration date.

**Premium:** To enter into an options contract, the trader must pay a Premium. Premiums are bought when entering the contract. A Premium acts as an insurance, protecting the trader against loss, if the ulterior move of the stock price moves in the opposite direction than expected. The trader loses no more than the amount they paid for the option - in this case, the premium.

## How a Call Trade Works

In the case of buying CALLS, if the price of the underlying stock does exceed the strike price, then the trader makes a profit. The amount of profit is the difference between the market price and option's strike price, multiplied by the increase in value of the underlying stock per share, minus the price paid for the option.

### Worked example

A trader pays a $150 premium for a stock option which is for 100 shares of the underlying stock. The strike price on such a stock is $25. On the option's expiration date, the shares are selling for $32.

- The buyer/holder has the right to hold 100 shares of such stock at $25 per share.
- When the value of the underlying stock reaches a certain point, the trader sells the 100 shares at the current market price of $32 per share.
- The investor's purchase price was $25 x 100 = $2,500.
- The investor's selling price is $32 x 100 = $3,200.
- The profit from the option is $700, minus the $150 premium paid for the option.
- Thus, the net profit becomes $700 − $150 = **$550**.

This is one of the distinguished advantages of option trading: for a limited investment, the buyer fastens uncapped profit potential with a collateralized and limited potential loss.

## A Word of Caution

Please be wary that the leading edge in options trading that causes your capital to grow quickly can also make you lose your capital just as fast.
